## 1. THE MENTAL MODEL — YOU ARE NOT SELLING

The best high-ticket closers in the world share a posture: **they are helping the prospect realize they already want this.** The prospect booked the call. The prospect sat down with a stranger for 20-30 minutes. The prospect is already telling you something by being in the room.

Selling = pushing. Closing = removing friction between where they are and where they've already decided they want to go.

This posture shift changes everything — tone, question order, how you handle objections, when you say the price.

Research base:
- **Neil Rackham, *SPIN Selling* (1988):** Situation → Problem → Implication → Need-payoff question order. Implication questions are the largest single predictor of close rate in complex sales.
- **Matthew Dixon & Brent Adamson, *The Challenger Sale* (2011):** 40% of top performers are "Challengers" who teach, tailor, and take control. Not pushy — sharp.
- **David Sandler, *You Can't Teach a Kid to Ride a Bike at a Seminar* (1995):** Pain funnel (surface → business → personal), upfront contracts, no-pressure closing.
- **Chris Voss, *Never Split the Difference* (2016):** tactical empathy, labeling emotions, mirroring, "that's right" moments. FBI hostage negotiation applied to sales.
- **Jordan Belfort, *Way of the Wolf* (2017):** Straight Line — three certainties (product, company, rep). Use the structure; do NOT use the tactics that feel manipulative.
- **Alex Hormozi, *$100M Offers* (2021):** value equation — (dream outcome × likelihood) / (time delay × effort). Use to present the offer.
- **Grant Cardone, *The Close* (2019) / Nick Kozmin (2020s high-ticket content):** modern $1K-$10K close motions for SaaS and services.

## 2. CALL STRUCTURE — THE 9-BEAT FLOW (20-30 MIN)

| Beat | Minutes | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Open | 0-2 | Rapport. Human beat. Agenda-set. |
| 2. Upfront contract | 2-3 | Sandler-style — "here's how this call will go" |
| 3. Situation | 3-6 | Light context questions, confirm setter briefing |
| 4. Problem | 6-10 | Pain questions — what's broken |
| 5. Implication | 10-14 | Cost of inaction — dollars, hours, stress |
| 6. Need-payoff | 14-16 | "If this was fixed, what changes?" |
| 7. Solution / teach | 16-22 | Tailored solution framing + teach moment |
| 8. Price + close | 22-27 | Price delivered flat, then close |
| 9. Payment + contract on call | 27-30 | Payment link live, signed before hang-up |

Do not rush. Do not skip a beat. Prospects who speed through Problem + Implication close at roughly half the rate of those who dwell there (Gong 2023 conversation intelligence data, directional — applies to B2B 15-60 min calls).

## 3. THE OPENING — FIRST 2 MINUTES

Frame: **peer, not vendor.** Two professionals solving a thing.

```
"Hey {{first_name}} — {{closer_name}} here. Thanks for grabbing the time. Before we dig in — how's your day actually going? Not the small-talk version, the real one."
```

Wait. Listen. If they're stressed, acknowledge. If they're good, good. Don't force script-y chitchat.

Then:
```
"Alright — I've got about 25 minutes with you. Here's what I'd love to do: first 5-10 minutes I want to understand what's going on in your business — what prompted the call, what's working, what isn't. Then I'll walk you through what we'd actually do for you specifically, and if it makes sense for both of us, we'll talk about next steps. If it doesn't, we'll shake hands and part friends. Cool?"
```

This is the **Sandler upfront contract**. It sets expectations, earns permission to ask hard questions, and — critically — explicitly allows "no" as an outcome. Paradoxically, granting the "no" door raises close rates (documented across Sandler + Voss training literature).

## 4. DISCOVERY — SPIN + CHALLENGER HYBRID

### 4.1 Situation (3-4 quick questions)

Confirm the setter briefing. Don't ask things you already know — it signals you didn't read the notes.

```
"Setter mentioned you're running {{niche}} in {{city}} and the team is {{team_size}} — still accurate?"
"Tell me in one sentence — what's the business do and who's the main customer?"
"What's the #1 source of new business right now — referrals, ads, walk-ins?"
```

### 4.2 Problem questions — where it hurts

```
"Walk me through what happens when a new lead comes in at 8pm on a Tuesday."
"How do you know when a lead falls through the cracks — or do you?"
"What have you tried before to fix this? How'd it go?"
"On a scale of 1-10 where 10 is 'this is actively costing me money every day,' where is this?"
```

The 1-10 question is a Voss-style **calibrated question**. It forces the prospect to quantify their own pain — which they then have to justify to themselves.

### 4.3 Implication — the cost of doing nothing

This is where most closers collapse. They move to solution too fast. Stay here. Make it hurt a little. Then keep going.

```
"If you keep doing it the way you're doing it now, what does that look like in 6 months?"
"You mentioned {{specific_pain}} — what's that costing you? Ballpark."
"Beyond the money — what's the personal cost? Working weekends? Not trusting your staff?"
"If you could wave a wand and fix just this one thing — what else opens up?"
```

The last question bridges into Need-payoff. Voss calls this the "vision of success." Hormozi calls it "dream outcome." Same move.

### 4.4 Need-payoff — their mouth, their words

```
"So if we could get you to a place where every lead — 24/7 — gets a call back in under 5 minutes, qualified, booked, and in your CRM with zero effort from you or the team... what does that do for you?"
```

Listen. Do not interrupt. Take notes on exact phrases. You'll use them in the close.

**The "that's right" moment:** Voss's signal that the prospect feels understood. When they say "yeah, that's exactly it" or "100%, that's the problem" — you've earned the right to present the solution. Before that, don't.

## 5. SOLUTION + TEACH — CHALLENGER STEP

Now you present. Not 20 slides. A 6-minute conversation.

### 5.1 Tailored framing

Don't list features. Frame the solution around **exactly** what they just told you.

```
"Okay — so based on what you said, here's what I'd actually build for {{company_name}}.

Problem 1 — {{their_specific_pain_1}}. We'd solve that with {{specific_bot_or_automation}}.

Problem 2 — {{their_specific_pain_2}}. That's {{specific_bot_or_automation}}.

And the reason I'm recommending this specific setup over the bigger one is — {{specific_reason_tied_to_their_situation}}. You don't need the full Scale package. You need these three things done well."
```

Crucial: **recommend the smaller appropriate package when it fits.** Recommending down builds trust that survives the close. Recommending up always feels pushy.

### 5.2 The Teach moment — Challenger

Drop ONE insight they didn't have when they joined the call. Something about their business, their niche, or their market. This is the single move that separates Challengers from average closers (Dixon/Adamson documented ~40% of top performers run this pattern).

Examples by niche:

- **Dental:** "Most dentists don't realize this — the single highest correlation to 5-star Google reviews isn't the quality of care, it's response speed on inquiry. 4.9 → 4.7 is almost always a response-time problem."
- **Plumbing:** "The thing that kills plumbing revenue isn't competition — it's your 'next plumber in Google' effect. Every missed call in the first 4 minutes has a 70% chance of booking with someone else (InsideSales / HBR 2011)."
- **Realtor:** "Zillow's internal data — top 10% agents respond in under 5 min. Close rate on those leads is 7x the 24-hour responders. That's the only reason speed matters."
- **Mortgage:** "Big banks retention-text your renewal customer at day 120. If you're not in the conversation by day 90, you've lost."

Ryan (Research) verifies every stat before a closer uses it in a live call.

### 5.3 Social proof — selectively

Drop ONE specific case study if it's truly relevant. Not three. Not a lineup. Example:

```
"The closest match I've got to your setup — {{comparable_client}}, {{niche}} in {{city}}. They started 90 days ago. First booking came through day 2 — a dog-walking lead at 11pm on a Tuesday. They now average {{X}} after-hours bookings a month."
```

Every case study used in a close has a signed testimonial release on file. PIPEDA + basic ethics.

## 6. PRICE — HOW TO DELIVER

### 6.1 The principle — never apologize, never hedge

Wrong:
> "So... the investment on this is, um... $997 for setup and $297 a month... which I know sounds like a lot but..."

Right:
> "Setup is $997 one time, monthly is $297. That's the number. What I want to make sure is — does that feel aligned with where you expected this to land?"

Flat delivery. Then pause. Let them talk. Most closers lose deals in the first 3 seconds after the price by filling silence with hedging.

### 6.2 Stack value BEFORE quoting — not after

Hormozi's value equation:
> **Perceived Value = (Dream Outcome × Likelihood) / (Time Delay × Effort)**

Before you say the number, make the numerator huge and the denominator tiny:

```
"Okay — so let's zoom out.

You told me you're losing ~{{$X}}/month in unanswered leads. That's the ceiling.
We'd get this live in 10-14 days — not 6 months.
Zero effort on your side once onboarded — my team does the setup, your team keeps doing their job.
3-month minimum — so the risk is bounded. Month 4 onward, month-to-month.

Setup is $997 one-time. Monthly is $297. Investment-to-payback is typically 30-60 days
based on what other {{niche}} shops see."
```

The order matters. Value first. Then price. Then silence.

### 6.3 Never lead with price

If the prospect tries ("just tell me how much so I know"), redirect:
```
"Totally fair — and I will, in about 2 minutes. I just need 90 seconds more to make sure the number I quote is actually the right number for YOUR setup, not a generic one. Cool?"
```

## 7. OBJECTION HANDLING — THE CLOSE

Every objection on a high-ticket call is one of **four** flavors. Map first, respond second.

| Objection family | What they actually mean |
|---|---|
| Price | "I don't see the value yet" |
| Time | "I'm not sure now" |
| Fit / trust | "I'm not sure it'll work for ME" |
| Authority | "I need someone else's buy-in" |

### 7.1 "It's too expensive"

This is **always** a value problem, not a price problem. The move: quantify value vs cost.

```
"I hear you. Let me ask you this — you said earlier you're losing ~${{X}}/month in unanswered leads.
Setup is $997 one-time, monthly is $297. That's $3,564 in year one.
If this recovers even 20% of that lost revenue, you're already ahead — and most of our clients
see 3-5x that in the first 90 days.

Is the number the issue, or is it that you're not 100% sure it'll actually work for you?"
```

The last question is Voss-style **labeling** — naming the emotion. 8 times out of 10, "too expensive" decodes to "I'm not sure it'll work." Which is a fit/trust objection, which you solve with a different move (see 7.3).

### 7.2 "I need to think about it"

Classic stall. Don't attack it. Dissect it.

```
"Totally fair. Most people who say that are actually telling me one of three things:
one, the money is a stretch right now — is that it?
two, they're not 100% sure this is the right fit — is that it?
three, they want to check with somebody — is that it?
Which one is it?"
```

Now you have a real objection to handle instead of a vague one. This is a Sandler-derived technique, widely reproduced in modern high-ticket training (Kozmin, Hormozi, etc.).

### 7.3 "I'm not sure this is right for us" / "I need to see it work"

This is fit/trust. Don't argue it. Reduce the risk.

```
"Fair — and I'd be worried if you WEREN'T asking that. Here's how we handle it:
the 3-month minimum is the only commitment. If after 60 days of live running,
you can tell me this isn't producing — we part ways at month 3 and I refund your last month.
No fine print, no arguing. That fair?"
```

Ask Connor + Lindsay BEFORE using refund language — confirm what Renée actually offers. Don't invent a guarantee live on a call.

### 7.4 "Send me more info, I'll review and get back"

Classic closer graveyard. Response:

```
"Yeah I can send a recap — but honest answer, info-in-inbox converts pretty poorly because
it's static. Everything we just talked about is specific to you. What would actually help —
is it that you want to re-review what we covered, or is there a specific question you feel
we haven't resolved yet?"
```

If there's a real specific question, answer it now. If it's a stall, name it:
```
"Got it. I'm hearing you're not quite ready to decide today. Totally fair — can I ask, what's
the specific thing you need between now and a decision? And can we put a date on it right
now so it doesn't drift?"
```

Book a follow-up on the call, with a clear agenda. Open-ended "I'll get back to you" = 80%+ loss (Gong).

### 7.5 "I'll call you back"

Never accept without a scheduled time.
```
"Cool — let's put it on the calendar so it doesn't fall off. Tuesday at 2 or Thursday at 10?"
```

### 7.6 "I need to talk to my business partner / spouse / accountant"

This is real AND a stall. Handle both:
```
"Completely get it. Two things — one, can we book a 15-min call with both of you so I can
answer whatever questions they'll have in real time instead of a game of telephone?
And two, on your side — if they're comfortable, are YOU comfortable? Because if this
makes sense to you, I'd rather build that alignment together than leave you to sell it."
```

Ideally: book a three-way follow-up within 72 hours. Otherwise: send a 2-minute video recap they can forward.

## 8. CLOSING MOVES — THE ACTUAL ASK

### 8.1 Summary close (the default — works in 70% of situations)

```
"Okay — quick recap before I let you go.
You said the biggest pain is {{pain_1}}. We solve that.
You said it's costing you {{$X}}/month. This gets it back.
Setup $997, monthly $297, 3-month min, live in 10-14 days.
You ready to get started?"
```

Then silence. Let them say yes, no, or the real objection.

### 8.2 Alternative choice close

When they're clearly ready but might flinch at "yes":
```
"Do you want to start with the Essentials setup we talked about, or does the Starter
fit what you need better?"
```

Either choice = a sale.

### 8.3 Assumptive close

Late-stage, momentum established:
```
"Cool — let me fire you the payment + signing link while we're still on. One sec."
```

This works *only* when you've earned it. Used too early, it's pushy.

### 8.4 Ben Franklin close

Used when they genuinely need to weigh tradeoffs:
```
"Okay — let's do this together. List reasons to do it, and list reasons to hold off.
Start with reasons to do it — you go first."
```

They'll list 4-6. You ask "what else?" 2-3 times. Then "reasons to hold off" — they'll list 1-2. Visible asymmetry closes it. Old-school but still works in consultative B2B.

### 8.5 The "no" close

When genuinely stuck:
```
"Can I ask — is there anything I could say on this call that would make you decide today?
If the answer is no, I'd rather know now than chase you for 3 weeks. No offense taken."
```

Reversing posture. Sometimes opens the real objection. Sometimes exits the deal faster — which saves both sides time.

## 9. CONTRACT + PAYMENT ON THE CALL

**The rule: if they say yes, you do NOT hang up until they've signed and paid.**

"I'll review and get back to you" = loss 70%+ of the time, even after a yes (industry rule of thumb — Gong 2024 consistent).

### 9.1 The live-close workflow

1. "Amazing. I'm sending you the agreement + Stripe link right now — one link does both. Give me 30 seconds."
2. Share screen OR send via email/SMS while on call
3. Walk through the agreement line-by-line quickly (30 seconds — see `easy-deploy-legal-disclaimer.md` for what's in it)
4. "Any questions on any of this?"
5. Wait for signature + payment confirmation notification
6. "Perfect, I see it came through. Thank you."

If they can't sign live (needs partner, needs to get card): agree on a specific return time (ideally within 24 hours). Send a confirmation SMS the moment the call ends with a direct link. Block 10 min on your calendar to re-engage at the promised time.

### 9.2 What the link does (built by Devon / Norm)

- Stripe Checkout Session for setup fee
- Stripe Subscription for monthly
- E-sign field (Documenso, DocuSeal, or similar)
- On success webhook → GHL workflow triggers onboarding
- Full legal disclaimer block (see `easy-deploy-legal-disclaimer.md`)

## 10. POST-CLOSE — 60 SECONDS THAT MATTER

### 10.1 Confirm next steps

```
"Okay — you're in. Here's what happens next:
— You'll get an onboarding email within an hour from {{onboarding_email}}
— Kickoff call is booked for {{day_time}} with {{onboarding_lead}}
— Between now and then, you'll get one form to fill out — takes 5 minutes
That's it. Welcome aboard."
```

### 10.2 The referral ask (in the same breath)

The best moment to ask for a referral is 30 seconds after someone buys. They're in the peak-commitment state. Don't skip it.

```
"One more thing while I've got you — who's the first person who came to mind when we were talking
just now? Someone in {{niche}} or similar who's dealing with the same thing? I'd love an intro if
you're open to it, no pressure."
```

Referral close rates are 3-5x cold close rates (Nielsen 2012, reproduced across SaaS vendor reports since). A referral ask at the post-close moment costs you 60 seconds and recovers 1 in 5 closers' pipeline.

### 10.3 Celebrate

Send a quick personal note within an hour (text or email). Not templated.
```
"{{first_name}} — pumped to have you onboard. Anything comes up between now and kickoff,
you've got my direct number. Talk soon."
```

## 11. CANADIAN-SPECIFIC — WHAT CLOSERS MUST KNOW

### 11.1 Cooling-off periods

- **Ontario Consumer Protection Act:** 10-day cooling-off for certain consumer contracts (primarily door-to-door and distance contracts meeting specific criteria). B2B services contracts are generally exempt. Lindsay (Legal) confirms per deal.
- **BC Business Practices and Consumer Protection Act, Alberta Consumer Protection Act, Quebec Consumer Protection Act:** similar and different rules per province. For B2B sales under a signed service agreement, most are exempt — but confirm per province on first close.
- Do NOT promise "no cancellation period." Honor whatever is contractually in place and let Lindsay's template handle the language.

### 11.2 PIPEDA + record-keeping

- Every call recording requires consent. Disclose on call: "Heads up, I record these calls for training — cool?"
- Retain recordings minimum 90 days, delete if prospect didn't close (unless audit trail required). Retain closed-deal recordings for contract duration + 2 years (standard business record-keeping window).
- Contact data stays purpose-limited — don't use closer call data for marketing without separate consent basis.

### 11.3 FSRA (mortgage-targeted closes only)

- Never quote a specific rate on a call
- Never guarantee approval
- Every commitment discussed must route through OLS brokerage compliance before signing
- See `fsra-mortgage-advertising-compliance.md` and `morgan-mortgage.md`

### 11.4 CASL (for the follow-up sequences triggered by the close)

- Closed-deal customer = express consent for service-related CEM
- Upsell/cross-sell messaging still needs a consent basis — usually the onboarding agreement covers it
- Unsubscribe in every CEM footer

## 12. METRICS — THE CLOSER DASHBOARD

| Metric | Benchmark | Red flag |
|---|---|---|
| Show rate (booked → showed) | 70-85% | Under 60% (setter problem) |
| Show → close rate | 20-40% on cold-sourced calls, 40-60% on warm | Under 15% |
| Average deal size (setup + 3mo) | Easy Deploy default: ~$1,888 | Trending down without niche explanation |
| Days to close after first call | Under 7 on true high-ticket | Over 21 — pipeline stall |
| Payment-on-call rate | 40-60% (industry avg for $1-10K) | Under 25% |
| Refund/chargeback rate | Under 2% | Over 5% — offer or expectation problem |

Research references: Gong Labs 2024, HubSpot Sales Benchmark 2023, Salesloft State of Sales 2024, Rev.io SaaS close data (directional).

### When to recalibrate

- Show → close under 15% for 10+ calls: Sam rides 3 live calls, audits full funnel
- Payment-on-call under 25%: revisit live-close workflow (§9)
- Days-to-close creeping past 14: follow-up discipline broken — see Sam's 6-step follow-up in `sam.md`
- Refund rate over 5%: offer promises something it doesn't deliver — Kai + Sam do offer review

## 14. RED LINES — NEVER CROSS

1. Never guarantee specific revenue, leads, appointments, or outcomes
2. Never quote a mortgage rate (FSRA)
3. Never lie about being human when asked (CRTC)
4. Never skip the upfront contract — it changes the whole call
5. Never invent a refund or guarantee live on a call — use what's in the contract template
6. Never pressure-close past a firm "no" — it backfires + damages brand
7. Never record a call without consent disclosure
8. Never accept "I'll get back to you" without a dated follow-up booked
9. Never close without Connor's signed-off offer + Lindsay's approved contract language
10. Never ask for the referral before the close — ask 30 seconds after
